STREETWEAR

STREETWEAR IS A STYLE OF CASUAL CLOTHING THAT GAINED GLOBAL POPULARITY IN THE 1990S, EMERGING FROM THE FUSION OF AFRICAN-AMERICAN AND LATINO HIP-HOP FASHION IN NEW YORK WITH ELEMENTS OF CALIFORNIAN SURF CULTURE. IT EVOLVED TO INCORPORATE ELEMENTS OF SPORTSWEAR, PUNK, SKATEBOARDING, 1980S NOSTALGIA, AND JAPANESE STREET FASHION.

✦

Streetwear style is generally accepted to have been born out of the African-American and Latino New York City hip-hop culture of the late 1970s and early 1980s, mixed with elements of Los Angeles surf culture. Early streetwear in the 1970s and 1980s also took inspiration from the do-it-yourself aesthetic of punk, Japanese street fashion, and established legacy sportswear brands.

Likewise, Surfboard designer Shawn Stussy began selling printed T-shirts featuring the same trademark signature he placed on his custom surfboards. Initially selling the items from his own car, Stussy expanded sales to boutiques once his popularity increased. Then as sales peaked, Stüssy moved into exclusive sales to create product scarcity, which established streetwear's focus on T-shirts and exclusivity.

Timberland boots are an everyday shoe in streetwear. In the early 1990s, burgeoning record labels associated with popular hip-hop acts like Tommy Boy Records, Def Jam Recordings, and Delicious Vinyl began selling branded merchandise embroidered onto letterman and workwear jackets made by companies like Carhartt.

By mid-decade, influences included skateboarding and gangsta rap. Professional American sports franchises have had a significant impact on the scene, especially the New York Yankees, Los Angeles Raiders and Chicago Bulls caps and jackets, with their production of oversized team jerseys, as well as boots from The Timberland Company and the latest shoe design releases from Nike, Inc. Brand launches by the chief executives of record companies followed, with Russell Simmons of Def Jam launching his Phat Farm label, Sean Combs of Bad Boy with Sean John, and Jay-Z and Damon Dash of Roc-a-Fella Records launching Rocawear.

During the 1980s, designer Dapper Dan created early streetwear aesthetics in Harlem, New York by combining luxury branding with custom designs influenced by hip-hop, helping popularize logo-centered fashion that diverged from mainstream norms.

The Evolution of Streetwear:

Streetwear’s Rise to Mainstream and Global Influence

The Global Expansion of Streetwear

Streetwear originated in urban hubs like New York and Los Angeles but spread quickly worldwide. Cities like Tokyo, London, and Paris embraced street style, creating unique local scenes. Brands such as Supreme became international symbols through frequent limited drops that built demand globally. The movement resonated with diverse groups, appealing to fashion enthusiasts and youth subcultures. Staples like oversized hoodies, t-shirts, and track pants became universal markers of streetwear culture. This expansion was supported by the growing accessibility of brands online and in flagship stores worldwide.

Collaborations with High Fashion and Luxury Brands

The fusion of streetwear with luxury brands redefined both markets. Brands like Louis Vuitton partnered with Supreme, merging limited drops and urban aesthetics with high fashion craftsmanship. These collaborations elevated streetwear’s status while introducing exclusive street style to luxury consumers. High fashion houses such as Balenciaga and Off-White incorporated streetwear staples, including sneakers and oversized hoodies, into their collections. This cross-pollination blurred traditional fashion boundaries and attracted new audiences to both industries.
